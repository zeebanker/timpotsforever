First Days

Some came from across continents. Others walked in fearing the ruler. Every Timpot remembers the moment they first stepped through the gate.

I missed school for a week or so when I first joined — coming from Morton School in W. Lafayette, Indiana, the bag and notebooks and uniforms were all new to me.

— Babji Patnaik · 1965

I joined KG in 1966 at the new building. Recall meeting Ms J. Rao at first visit with parents. Like I was being checked in to a detention center 🤣. The boys had a play on kings and princes. They made me place a pillow to represent a fat prince. Can still play that role now … naturally 😝.

— Mahesh Allam · Neptune · joined KG 1966

I studied in St Joseph's till the 3rd standard and joined Timpany in the 4th standard in 1966. Tara Viswanathan was my teacher. I was in the class of Anantharam, James Moses, Jyotsna Chauhan and Parveen. Timpany did not have an ISC exam centre then — we had to go and write our exams in St Joseph's Girls High School.

— Joseph Polimetla · 1966

It was 1981. My Dad returned to India after post-doc research. Somebody suggested Timpany School. Things moved quickly and I was standing before Ms David. She was holding a wooden ruler. I would find out its purpose very soon. My dad walked me up to the class and hardly managed to say "this is Miss David, your new teacher" before he had to leave. I wanted to say hello, but she cut me off with a stern look.

Bhanu Pratap was sitting next to me when I was fumbling through my bag. I remember every word he said. "What ra, no syllabus book aa?" I looked at him imploringly. The next moment, Ms David was yelling. "Banu Pratap come here!" Poor chap knew what was coming, I didn't. Ms David smacked his hand three times with the wooden ruler. Each time he pulled his hand back only to put it back. I was already trembling — he was not.

That evening, I came down with a fever. I missed school for a week or so. The whole scene felt like the workhouse from Oliver Twist.

— Suri Chitti · joined Std II C in 1981

Walking with Jazz

"Miss Jeyaraja Rao" to the school records. "Big Miss" to the children. "Jazz" to those who dared — a nickname that, Siva Prasad notes, was in circulation by 1968, long before any Spielberg movie. These are direct encounters with her.

I was just a little kid when I first saw Miss Mercy. My parents came to join me in the BABY CLASS by the sea. Big Miss as I first saw her — elegant, gorgeous, with a divine smile, a look that was so gentle and a touch that felt so warm and kind.

— Aruna Patnaik · Pluto · 1959–1970

We came all the way to school from Shipyard. Sometimes the main gates were closed (for late comers) — we could hear the assembly, the Amen, a thought or a little story for the day, then a pause. We knew Big Miss was stepping out to address us. A finger shakes at us. A thunder breaks out. The sky falls. On the other side of the gate, our legs go weak and tremble. We feel faint. No excuses were allowed. Miss knew better. I think I even squinted in fear.

— Aruna Patnaik · Pluto · 1959–1970

My first day at Timpany in 6th grade began with an intercom announcement from Ms Jeyaraja Rao — "Children you should all have a clean hair cut … if not I will ensure to bring scissors and personally cut your hair." Me being continuously balded as a child each summer, finally had two strands of hair growing on my head and getting to dream of pleating up my hair and putting garlands of Jasmine. Hearing the announcement traumatized my first day. (Did not realize it was meant for boys only 🤣🤣.) I kept profusely crying.

New student crying for half a day was taken to Ms Jazz's office finally. I remember my first conversation with her so vividly — how kind she was. She sat me in her lap, reassuring that my life will be ok and I can dream big — long hair 🤣 or perhaps any big and impossible dreams.

— Sridevi Kurella · 1989 ICSE

Jazz had a brilliant mind and very progressive for those days. I remember she let my daughter skip her final exams so she could sail with her father. She said travelling was an education in itself. So very grateful to her for that.

— Bharati Sikand

When I was in a precarious, absolutely hopeless medical situation in 2010, I reached out to Miss J for advice. After a reply from Miss J — who got a set of the best medical doctors and surgeon in the US — I was winging my way to the US for surgery soon. Miss J and Sandy met me at the airport in Tennessee and stayed with me for 3 days post surgery. Even my family was not around during those tenuous times. I am thankful to God for having brought Miss J into my life.

— Piyush "Mickey" Nair · Neptune · Class of 1978

When Miss J's white Ambassador car would arrive at the school, there would be signals from the old bathrooms that Miss J was arriving, as the entrance to the school had to be cleared, everybody in class, all still and quiet — a lull before the storm 😂.

— Piyush "Mickey" Nair · Neptune

We still remember how 7:55 a.m. was never just a time — it was a line you dared not cross. Walking in late felt like entering a lion's den, where her sharp words would shake you up, but also set you right for life. There were moments that stayed with us forever — students made to stand in the corridor for untidy uniforms, reminders about polished shoes, trimmed nails, proper haircuts, and carrying a handkerchief every day.

— Rayudu

The bold and the daring among us would call out "Jazz is coming" and scoot, but for the darpoks like me, it was and is still Big Miss. I'd melt on seeing her, fumble for words, before she gives the reassuring "Hello George."

— T George John

I was caught at the gate due to late coming a good number of times 😅. All the more, Miss Jeyaraja Rao remembers me!

— V J Gupta · 1978–80

I still remember the most embarrassing trip our class had with Jazz to watch a very beautiful film on Friendship. The film was called "Friends" and Bo Derek was not friendly to us 😜🫣. Jazz went red. The rest is history 🤪.

— Dr Meenakshi Anantram · Jupiter Captain · ISC 1980

The Teachers We Remember

Beyond Big Miss, a faculty whose names alumni still whisper decades later. Kalim Ali calls them "jewels." For a fuller directory of names, see School History → Our Teachers.

Mrs Asha Noori, a sweet elderly lady who taught English, was the first teacher I remember who taught us e.nun.ci.a.tion and diction beyond just pronunciation of words. I still remember the day when I suggested the word "gauche" when she was prompting the class for alternative words, and she made sure the entire class applauded me (even though I didn't know how to pronounce the word correctly).

— Sarat Chandra

Mrs Tara teacher and Mrs Milan, such a sweet chubby lady! How she used to correct our pronunciation, unforgettable. The vowel pronunciation, she would say fold your lower lip in shape of 'V' and say (in her own words) "What Wawa … Wawa … you speak as though you have a hot potato in your mouth! Open your mouth, speak up!"

— Patta Saroj · Neptune · 1974 ISC

On Teachers' Day, I was assigned to teach Miss Melbourne's class. I was having a hard time controlling the class. She came in and said "Quiet" in a voice that turned everyone to stone instantly. I learned a very important lesson that day! My mouth used to water at the thought that the students baked cakes inside the building!

— Sekhar T (Chandra Sekhar)

I remember Pantulu garu (Sri Tatachari) who taught us Sanskrit in class V, 1968. He would even throw the duster at us if we weren't paying attention. And his favourite name for weak students (me) was "you single digit rascal."

— Shankar Mathur · Pluto Captain · ISC 1975

"George will you kindly shut up" — Mrs Elisha's trademark way of silencing the talkative ones 😂. Miss would also peep over the window to see which of us were fidgeting while class was going on. Got the taste of straps on my hand once, but then Miss was particular that all the t's were crossed and the i's had the dots — to the last detail of every programme.

— T George John

After school all the teachers became aunties — Reuben aunty, Chiranjeevi aunty and uncle, and so forth. The year I joined the KG school was for full day and we even had nap time after lunch.

— Rama Davarapalli

Most of the teachers in our times were jewels! I wonder how she managed to get the best teachers without an HR department, like in modern times. I can't remember one teacher who was unfit for the post. Yes, English was one of the main strengths of Timpany those days. Handwriting was a big problem for most of us — they were very strict about it.

— Kalim Ali

Having been a teacher for the past 32 years, I've aped almost all my teachers from Timpany. I use the phrase "Nasty Children" exactly like her 🤪. And I love saying that.

— Sri Susarla · ISC 1986

I can do all kinds of stitches by hand, thanks to Mrs Chitra. And often do my own needlework at home.

— Sridevi Adimulam · ISC 1993

In school my teachers thought of me as intelligent but naughty, maybe unruly too. In my adulthood, people I have worked with have called me a very cultured person. I owe it to these women — I was moulded in the cultural womb they collectively spawned.

— Suri Chitti

Mr. Chiranjeevi

PT instructor, NCC under-officer, four-musketeer of the CBM Compound — and somehow, every back-bencher's favourite invigilator. Sir gets his own section.

I had a great friendship with Mr Chiranjeevi. He supported me and protected me during my days in the Indian Handball camp for the Asian Games. I played for the country, but every time I had to take permission from Jazz to attend the camps outside Vizag it was so difficult. She would be so upset with me and thought I would do badly in academics. But Sir was my saviour. Always feel indebted to him. I donned the Indian Cap with pride.

— Dr Meenakshi Anantram · Jupiter Captain · ISC 1980

Mr. Chiranjeevi was every back-bencher's favourite exam invigilator. I'm sure teachers saw 10 copies of everything for any exam he monitored, because he just said "What ra! Don't copy!!" and that was it. Free for all afterwards.

— KP (K Prabhakar) · Pluto · ISC 1990

We had PT Sir who planned every game so meticulously — he brought in all his years of experience as an NCC under officer to train us on par with them, with commands "Bhaise Thez chal," Vishram, Savdhan, "Thaine mod bhaye mod," "Piche mod."

— Evangeline Sushma · 1990 ICSE

I remember the grand sports day we had outside of the campus, jubilee celebrations and the races and the excitement, jumping with Sharon through the fire hoop with Mr Chiranjeevi watching us like a hawk and taking care of us.

— Rama Davarapalli

The four musketeers of CBM Compound — Mrs Chiranjeevi, Cormaty, Reuben and Nair — would often meet each other on a personal level regularly every week. And they all stayed within a stone's throw of each other.

— Piyush "Mickey" Nair · Neptune

Houses, Sports & the Field

Jupiter (blue), Mercury (yellow), Neptune (green), Pluto (red — later Venus). Throwball, baseball, the pole vault, the long-awaited shield. The field is where the heart still beats fastest.

How I miss the battlefields where the Neptune, Pluto, Jupiter and Mercury houses sent their warriors to DO or DIE!!! Such intensity of courage, valour, strength and honour of our team and for the glory of sports!!

— Evangeline Sushma · 1990 ICSE

Throwball — I recall that in the early 70s the staff used to play throwball with the winners of that year. They had to play with the Jupiter team (my team). They lost for the first time against the students and they never played after that for … I don't know. Never kept track. We were very happy that we beat the lady staff. I still have the aluminium school box.

— Annapurna Banerjee · 1972 ISC

One day, we had a throwball match against the Teachers. That was the day I saw a side of Mrs Reuben Ma'am I had never seen before. Quietly, she stepped onto the court, tucked in her saree and palloo, took the front-centre position, and gave us students the toughest match of our lives 😎. Needless to say, the Teachers' team won that day.

— Monisha Chakravarthy Hatkar · ISC 1994

Took me back to that historic day in 1978 when Jupiter House (finally 😂) won Throwball after many years and as Captain and Vice Captain me and Nikhil Oza (better known as Ozone 😍) escaped to pick treats for our victorious girls and got caught jumping the wall near the toilets by "the Jazz." Met the stick first time.

— Dr Meenakshi Anantram · Jupiter Captain · ISC 1980

Anyone from my batch will vividly remember Peter D'Brass and his incident with the pole vault. We stayed back after school one day and he was practising (without permission or supervision) when the tip of the pole ripped through his cheek and came out just below his eye. After that all the equipment was locked up after school. Jazz would remember this incident and how much she prayed for Peter.

— Sandhya Gautam · ISC 1976

I was a very little girl when I was in awe with Hazari doing the pole vault in school. Whenever I heard his name on the mic from the Lower Terrace, I'd have my eyes glued towards the back gate corner to see him run with the pole and fly over the crossbar. Never saw any school have pole vaulting in this part of the world.

— Sri Susarla · ISC 1986

Mercury house, lifelong loyalist.

— Pari (Paripurna)

Songs, Stages & Annual Day

Christmas pageants. Drums of Freedom. India and Its States. Birthday songs for Big Miss. And a school song whose lyrics have travelled, slightly changed, across half a century.

The Drums of Freedom was in '85 or '86 — something with dances from all the Indian states, for which I was the emcee.

— Aarti Samuel (Boaz) · 1982–86

Does anyone remember the "Drums of Freedom" or "India and Its States," the performances by the entire school? Brings back a flush of nostalgic memories from Timpany school.

— Sridevi Kurella · 1989 ICSE

I was a Maharashtrian bride in the Brides of India tableau — feeling very shy to turn around to show the attire because of the 'tuck' of the sari at the back. Such a funny, awkward age!

— Aruna Patnaik · Pluto · 1959–1970

I was part of the States of India. Mrs Vaz draped me in Bengali-style saree. I am not just reminiscing the times — I feel I am living it.

— Sucharita Narasipuram · 1977–1985

Annual Day … "Dekho re dekho, dar gayo paapi, bichuaa" — choreographed by our Hindi Teacher Mrs Nair. Christmas Pageant. Girl gang … ISC, batch of 1974.

— Jabeen Marwaha (Mehdi)

For Timpany's Golden Jubilee we sang: "Fifty years of Timpany's progress we celebrate now this year / God has been our guide and refuge ever faithful ever true …"

My best and favourite choir song was for Big Miss's birthday every year: "It's a hap hap happy birthday / May you live to be a hundred and three."

— Evangeline Sushma · 1990 ICSE

"Love in any language, comes straight from the heart / It pulls us all together, never ever apart / And once we learn to speak it, all the world will hear / Timpany School's love for children, fluently spoken here." This was a song that we as the school choir along with Jeffrey Kirk composed along with Jazz Miss and Sandy Wisener for Timpany's 70th anniversary.

— Rahul Ganapati

We hold our banner high / For honour we aspire / Come join the songs of triumph loud / Of Timpany we are proud!

— Neeharika · Mercury House Captain · ICSE 2002

We had to sing/dance to "Dulan gachi Jo Jo Jhoom" — a Santhal song. Then another time Mrs Syed had us sing "Zara sambhal sambhal kar chalna." And "Dhitaang dhitaang bole" was another song.

— Sandhya Gautam · ISC 1976

Mischief & Misadventures

The dark room. The shortened skirts. Frogs escaping in the science lab. Boys scattering when the white Ambassador appeared at the gate. Confessions, decades later.

There was a dark room alongside the cyclostyling machine room next to Miss J's office, guarded by Jung and Prasad Bahadur. The security was much better than Fort Knox. Often naughty students (and I was one of them) would be locked in the dark room.

Then as you moved up the ladder, punishment was kneel outside your class, and remain so until the recess break 😭. The famous leather strap on both palms was only used by the supreme leader, Miss J. Some teachers would use a wooden ruler on your reversed palm — "show me your knuckles" — and much more. So in essence being in Timpany was baptism by fire, no shortcuts, follow the process.

— Piyush "Mickey" Nair · Neptune

One of the year 11s was standing on the library table and making the speech of Mark Antony (from Julius Caesar). Miss Jeyaraja Rao walked in, but spared them a telling-off as she was impressed with the passion with which the speech was delivered.

— Shekinah Chavvakula (Ratnalatha) · 1975

That [Mark Antony speech] was our class. She stood at the door and we continued with Mark Antony's speech, not knowing she was there. Then we froze. I was on the table. Sushila Krishnamurthy was also with me on the table but she had the presence of mind to jump down. I didn't even have the presence of mind to jump down.

— Lucy Rachel

Satyendra, myself and David Swaroop Raj made a tree house on the beach road, where there were mud ravines, and used to pack sandwiches on Sundays and spend our time in the branches. Lovely days!

— Kalim Ali

A group of 3 girls — respectable grandmothers now 😊 — quietly shortened their skirts considerably over the summer holidays. Never got caught! Because the boys never sneaked / snitched … and probably because they were the only ones who noticed such things, better than the teachers!

— Ravi Mamidanna · Jupiter · Class of '73

I remember Miss Joy in the lab — once the boys left the jar which had the frogs open, and when she lifted the lid, they escaped all over the lab! 😁 Another time the boys opened the Kipp's apparatus and the awful smell of hydrogen sulphide filled the room — lab cancelled! 😂

— Uju (Ujwala S)

We boys were chased away from the landing — someone would spot the car coming down Maharashtra Mandal road, and all of us scurrying to the nearest classroom — yet some of us got caught. Somehow Neptune got all the best sportsmen right from Damodar's days.

— T George John

I was one of the guys that got warned of a haircut in the class. Now I don't need to worry about haircuts 😛.

— Rama Gogineni · ICSE 1989

Campuses, Uniforms & Haunts

The Dutch-looking building. The CBM Compound. Asilmetta. Maroon skirts and khaki trousers. Kit Kat, Valex Soman, Gupta Brothers (since 1916). The texture of every Timpot's daily life.

Was an old Dutch-looking building near the present "big school" — went from KG to Class 2 there, then they made new buildings. I always got Big Miss a flower for her hair from my mother's garden. Left her one today 💐.

— Pari (Paripurna) · Mercury

By any chance, can we get a picture of the very, very old castle-like building of our Timpany Small School? We had our KG to 2nd std there.

— Rama (K V Rama Devi) · Jupiter · '85 / '87

The building itself, the old one in Timpany Junior School, was not very assuring either. Today, I miss those buildings, but at 7 years old, the whole scene felt like the workhouse from Oliver Twist.

— Suri Chitti · 1981

Boys had to be at least 5ft tall or be a student of Grade 11 to wear trousers instead of shorts 😊. Girls on the other hand had to wear skirts, no shorter than a couple of inches above the knee. By 10th standard, I could break the rules and get away with it. Was still tiny in Grade 11 😅. Boys — khaki trousers. Girls wore maroon skirts.

— Ravi Mamidanna · Jupiter · Class of '73

I wish someone could organize a Timpany School Walk Down Memory Lane — covering the school and old haunts like Valex Soman, Gupta Brothers etc. And the fees used to be paid through Federal Bank during my time, with three counter foils.

— Aarti Samuel (Boaz) · 1982–86

Uplands Corner served some amazing items. I remember Renu's mother, Omana Paul, made laddoos for all the students of Timpany on one Independence Day when we did dances of the various states on the India map.

— Ganga Ramanathan (Narayan) · 1975

I run my family business of Gupta Brothers books — we started the shop in 1916 and still going on, crossed 100, I'm the third generation.

— V J Gupta · 1978–80

We lived right next to school and would hear the first bell and rush out of the house to be just in time before the second bell. Bahadur was the gateman whom we would meet up in holidays and Sundays.

— Sucharita Narasipuram · 1977–1985

Not to forget Ms Joy, our Lab Asst, Jung, security guard (Gurkha), Prasad, Sanyasi, ayammas Saraswathi and Savitri.

— Patta Saroj · Neptune · 1974 ISC

Mrs Kantha Morada was my Hindi teacher from Grade 3–5 too 👍 She lived in my street, Dwaraka Nagar in Vizag. We used to watch her pass by with her dark cooling glasses 😎 and umbrella 🌂.

— Laxmi Kumar

Always a Timpot

The name travelled. Chris remembers it being used as early as the train rides from Waltair Jn. in the 1960s. Sixty years and six continents later, it still finds people.

We used to travel to school every morning in train from Waltair Jn. to Vizagapatam station — both Aloysians and Timpanians in different compartments of Raipur Passenger. Even then we were known as Timpots.

— Chris (Christopher John Emandi) · born 20 Aug 1952

St Aloysius was exclusive boys, St Joseph's and Fort Convent for exclusive girls — we were the only co-ed convent in town, so were special right from the beginning to be groomed under the highly disciplinarian and dynamic "Jazz."

— Neelu Murthy · Mercury · 1963–70

Walked into Timpany school at the age of 6 yrs! Now we are 68 yrs! But our minds are still fresh with memories of our good old school and still we proudly call ourselves "TIMPOTS."

— Patta Saroj · Neptune · 1974 ISC

I met him and his wife on the last night of an 18-day cruise along the western coast of Africa, almost exactly a year ago — March 30, 2025. He said he was from Vizag and then of course the Timpany link was established. It was absolutely thrilling 🤩😍.

— Aarti Samuel (Boaz) · on meeting Peter D'Brass at sea

Although I am 75 and have left school long ago, when I have met Miss and a few of my old teachers a few times, I was pleasantly surprised that they recognised me.

— Babji Patnaik · 1965

We are in a time machine. Back in the last quarter of the 20th century. No computer, no mobile, no social media, no AI times. Everything well ingrained in the mind as memories 🥰.

— Sri Susarla · ISC 1986

We earned pots full of a, b, c, d, e … during our association with Timpany school and all that it represented. We imbibed something silently through our growing years.

— Annapurna Banerjee · 1972 ISC

Always a Timpot at heart, whichever corner of the earth we live in.

— Neeharika · Mercury House Captain · ICSE 2002
